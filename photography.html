Photography

Photography started out as a hobby. I found that I enjoyed taking pictures, so I took pictures. I didn't think much of it, and I didn't think that I wanted to be a professional photographer. After making the decision to invest in a real camera, I approached photography from a different angle. I decided that, while I didn't plan on becoming a professional photorapher, I did want to use my photorgraphy in my work. I began incorporating my pictures in my websites, and using them on class projects.

Instead of relying on stock images, I put my own images to use. This is much more convenient, because I can make my own images any resolution that I want, and I don'thave to worry about paying someone for the rights to use their images.

Photography isn’t a game for the faint of heart. I still haven’t fully explored the different settings on my camera, and I’ve learned to take my time and read the manual, and not be afraid to try something different. One of my favorite pictures was the result of taking a picture of something that was reflected in the glass of a camera lens that was sitting on my desk. I then took that image’s RAW file (the unprocessed file that a digital camera outputs) and edited it in Adobe Lightroom until I found the effect that I wanted. Lightroom has become an essential tool to me due to its exposure editing features – I don’t shoot with flash, and because of that sometimes my images look underexposed. Lightroom allows me to take that underexposed RAW and crank the exposure up to something that is presentable, without a loss in quality or an overabundance of noise (the multicolored spots that show up when a digital camera’s sensor overheats).

Patience and experimentation are the lessons learned on the path to photography. The best way is to start simple and work your way up to the pros. I’ve still got a lot to learn about photography, such as color theory and how it relates to landscape imagery, and that’s my favorite part about it. There’s always something new to try.
